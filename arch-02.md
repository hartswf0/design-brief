Excavating the Speculative Present: A Methodological and Socio-Technical Analysis of Design Fiction Artifacts
The Stratification of Speculative Environments
Design fiction operates at the critical intersection of speculative design, science fiction, and socio-technical forecasting. It utilizes narrative and tangible artifacts to interrogate the ethical, environmental, and structural implications of emerging technologies.1 Unlike traditional futurism, which often seeks to predict, optimize, or streamline a singular path forward, design fiction constructs an alternative present or near future to establish a provocative "New Normal".2 By bypassing the simplistic binaries of utopian progress or dystopian collapse, the discipline forces audiences to confront the mundane, operational, and often contradictory realities of technological integration.3 The core mechanism of this practice is the creation of the "diegetic prototype"—an object, interface, or service that exists natively within a fictional narrative but is rendered visceral and functional enough to provoke real-world socio-technological debate.2
An analytical and rigorous approach to design fiction requires treating these speculative artifacts not as mere narrative props, but as complex archaeological sites. A complete socio-technical excavation demands the stratification of every relevant layer of reality: the polished public-facing marketing, the messy operational friction of user onboarding, the subterranean secondary markets, the environmental degradation, and the historical context of the object.5 The true impact of a disruptive technology is rarely found in its intended, frictionless function. Instead, it is located in the friction of its physical deployment, the inevitability of its mechanical breakdown, the legal liability shields erected to protect its corporate creators, and its eventual commodification in the illicit grey market.6
This exhaustive analysis investigates four distinct idioms of design fiction—cinematic diegetic prototypes, repair manuals, quick start guides, and product catalogs—to map the profound disparities between technological promises and ground-level execution. By treating epistolary artifacts and found documents as evidentiary records, this report unpacks the complex labor dynamics, legal frameworks, and economic systems that define both speculative and contemporary infrastructures.5
The Theoretical and Methodological Foundations of Design Fiction
To fully comprehend the analytical power of design fiction, it is necessary to trace its origins and methodological frameworks. Originating from critical design—a practice pioneered by Anthony Dunne and Fiona Raby to critique the limitations of traditional, commercially focused design—speculative design evolved into a robust method for exploring how design influences societal and ethical concerns.1 Design fiction specifically emerged from the intersection of this critical practice and the narrative techniques of science fiction literature and cinema.1 As defined by Julian Bleecker, a foundational figure in the discipline, design fiction objects act as "totems through which a larger story can be told, or imagined or expressed".2 They serve as artifacts from an alternative reality, functioning as entry points into complex controversies surrounding artificial intelligence, biotechnology, ubiquitous computing, and transhumanism.2
However, the discipline has increasingly sought to move beyond the purely diegetic frame of cinematic worldbuilding.10 While early iterations of design fiction relied heavily on the "science of imaginary solutions" (pataphysics) and narrative storytelling, contemporary practitioners deploy these methods as rigorous academic and corporate research tools.3 The Near Future Laboratory, for example, utilizes design fiction to generate tangible organizational prototypes, deliberately eschewing traditional PowerPoint presentations in favor of creating fictional user manuals, annual reports from the future, or corporate videos that showcase the limitations and frictions of proposed technologies.2
This methodological shift is heavily utilized in contemporary academic research. At the Georgia Institute of Technology, researchers within the Creating Ethics Infrastructures Lab heavily integrate design fiction into their qualitative methodologies.12 Researchers such as Richmond Y. Wong utilize "Design Fiction Memos" to analyze the values work of User Experience (UX) professionals, product managers, and AI practitioners.12 By crafting infrastructural speculations, these researchers probe the gaps between how corporate ethics are imagined in public rhetoric and how they are actually executed in the grueling reality of software development.12 Wong's work explicitly explores how practitioners navigate ethical challenges, utilizing speculative artifacts to envision alternative ways to implement privacy, fairness, and security.12 These artifacts act as levers, testing the efficacy of legal and corporate policies in mitigating digital harm before those harms manifest in the real world.12
Cinematic Diegetic Prototypes and the Friction of Physicality
Cinematic environments serve as the most visible testing grounds for diegetic prototypes, embedding speculative technologies within the narrative fabric of film to observe their sociotechnical ramifications. However, the seamless presentation of future technologies on screen actively masks the intense labor, budgetary friction, and physical constraints required to produce them.14 Analyzing the pre-production, on-set execution, and post-production realities of these prototypes exposes the deep contradictions between the prop designer's physical build, the visual effects (VFX) team's digital overlay, and the technical advisor's compromised scientific reality.
Posthumanist Worldbuilding and the Illusion of Tangibility
The creation of a convincing diegetic prototype begins with a fundamental conflict between physical fabrication and digital extension. Production designers are tasked with grounding speculative concepts in a visceral reality, often drawing inspiration from historical aesthetics or naturalistic environments to avoid the cliches of hyper-advanced science fiction.16 For instance, the 2013 film Her, directed by Spike Jonze with production design by K.K. Barrett, utilized a highly specific approach to diegetic prototyping to explore a posthumanist future.16 To make the future feel "around the corner," the production actively avoided dystopian tropes or overtly futuristic hardware like drones and self-driving cars.16
Instead, Her utilized subtle adjustments to real-world locations, fusing the urban landscapes of Los Angeles with the elevated pedestrian walkways of Shanghai's Pudong district.16 These walkways served as a diegetic prototype for future urban infrastructure, portraying characters as comfortable pedestrians moving seamlessly through a post-anthropocentric grid.16 This design choice reflects a posthumanist relational ontology—drawing upon the theories of Karen Barad and Donna Haraway—where the spatial, ontological, and epistemological distinctions between human and nonhuman (AI) entities are deliberately blurred.16 The central diegetic prototype, the AI operating system "Samantha," operates with ubiquity and invisibility, reflecting Haraway's assertion that modern microelectronics muddle the distinction between physicality and invisibility.16
However, achieving this seamless integration requires immense physical and logistical negotiation. In productions like Captain America: Brave New World, production designer Ramsey Avery detailed the profound challenges of integrating practical set builds with VFX extensions.17 A primary technical hurdle is determining the placement of "cut lines"—the precise physical boundaries where practical construction ends and digital rendering begins.17 Avery noted that complex organic shapes, such as "lacy trees," make it exceedingly difficult for VFX teams to extend scenes seamlessly; consequently, denser foliage or solid architectural platforms must be utilized to create clean hand-offs.17 When previs (pre-visualization) teams design environments without consulting the art department, they frequently propose architectures that defy physical gravity, budget constraints, or the structural integrity of available materials.17
The physical execution of these environments often demands extraordinary engineering. In the production of Gravity, the desire to provide a physical rendering of "intangible spaces" led to the creation of a one-hundred-foot-long rotating drum and a complex performative lightbox.14 This allowed actors to be lit as if they were spinning in all axes while remaining stationary, providing an embodied, dimensional environment rather than a standard, haptically devoid blue-screen set.14
The Asymmetry of Labor and Post-Production Realities
The friction between the physical and the digital is most acutely felt in the labor dynamics of the VFX industry. The drive to create flawless diegetic prototypes has resulted in a global supply chain of digital labor characterized by short-term contracts, extreme margin compression, and pervasive exploitation.15 The bidding process for VFX work is structurally flawed; studios operate on razor-thin margins, absorbing the costs of endless directorial revisions and shifting technological demands to appease industry giants like Marvel Studios.15
The systemic fragility of this model was laid bare when Rhythm and Hues, the VFX studio responsible for the visually groundbreaking diegetic wildlife in Life of Pi, filed for bankruptcy just eleven days before winning the Academy Award for Best Visual Effects in 2013.15 A late-stage directorial change by Ang Lee required the studio to redo massive amounts of digital rendering at their own expense, effectively destroying their financial solvency.15 This contradiction—where the production of a multi-million-dollar speculative future bankrupts the very artists building it—serves as a vital subterranean layer of the cinematic artifact. The diegetic prototype is therefore not just a reflection of future technology, but a physical manifestation of contemporary labor exploitation.15
Furthermore, the introduction of virtual production environments, such as those powered by Unreal Engine, has fundamentally altered the epistemic authority of the production designer.18 The traditional hierarchy of set construction is flattened as the imaginative construction of the story-world is shifted to a digital package.18 The production designer is no longer the sole architect of the space; they become managers of the physical interface between the actor and the LED volume, attempting to sync real props with rotating virtual sets.18 Actors interacting with these prototypes often struggle against the lack of haptic feedback, resulting in script annotations and blocking notes that reflect a deep frustration with the physical limitations of the prop.14

Speculative Infrastructure, Degradation, and the Right to Repair
If cinematic diegetic prototypes explore the deployment of speculative technology, the idiom of the repair manual explores its inevitable degradation. Technology does not exist in a perpetual state of glossy functionality; it exists in a state of continuous decay, subject to environmental wear, human error, and the intentional obsolescence programmed by Original Equipment Manufacturers (OEMs).19 The repair manual, therefore, serves as a highly contested socio-political battleground between corporate control and user sovereignty. By investigating the tension between official documentation and underground workarounds, design fiction can excavate the fragility of modern infrastructure.
The Monopoly of Original Equipment Manufacturers
The modern speculative infrastructure is characterized by restrictive intellectual property (IP) regimes and Digital Rights Management (DRM) frameworks designed to prevent unauthorized maintenance.19 OEMs actively consolidate control over the lifecycle of their products by withholding diagnostic tools, locking firmware, and threatening to void warranties if a device is opened by an independent technician or the equipment owner.21 This dynamic fundamentally transforms the concept of ownership; end-users no longer possess their technology, they merely license the right to operate it under the strict parameters defined by the manufacturer.23
The official OEM workshop manual perfectly reflects this paradigm. It is a sterile, legally cautious document heavily sanitized to protect the corporation from liability while simultaneously reinforcing their monopoly.24 It dictates highly specific, often unnecessarily complex procedures that require proprietary tools, ensuring that the repair supply chain remains entirely within the OEM's profitable ecosystem.21 When global supply chains fracture—as they frequently do across multi-continent logistics networks—the user is left stranded, holding a backordered manifest while their critical infrastructure fails, completely barred from implementing their own repairs.24
Unauthorized Workarounds and the Black Market of Maintenance
The suppression of the right to repair inevitably spawns sophisticated subterranean networks of unauthorized maintenance.25 The agricultural sector provides a profound and highly visible real-world precedent for this phenomenon. Modern farming equipment, such as John Deere tractors, operates as complex sociotechnical systems containing millions of lines of proprietary source code.20 By classifying these vehicles as software ecosystems rather than mere mechanical tools, manufacturers utilized the Digital Millennium Copyright Act (DMCA) to effectively outlaw independent repair, arguing that modifying or bypassing the tractor's code constituted illegal copyright infringement.20
In response, farmers and independent mechanics established decentralized networks to distribute cracked firmware, often sourced from Eastern European hackers, to bypass the OEM's digital locks.25 These unauthorized workarounds—traded on invite-only, paid online forums—represent the raw, messy reality of technological survival.25 The grease-stained, handwritten notes passed between mechanics detailing how to bypass a localized climate-control tent or an autonomous drivetrain stand in stark contrast to the sterile, heavily encrypted OEM diagnostic logs.25
This environment fosters a unique form of technological agonism, where the end-user actively resists the technological enclosure enforced by the corporation.26 The tension between official systems and underground hacks reached a critical public juncture at the 2022 DEF CON security conference, where a hacker known as Sick.Codes demonstrated the ability to jailbreak John Deere 2630 and 4240 displays.23 By physically altering the circuit boards—soldering controllers directly onto the hardware to bypass dealer authentication requirements—the hacker gained full access to the root operating system.27 This exploit forced the system to surrender over 1.5 GB of proprietary diagnostic logs, proving that the digital enclosures constructed by OEMs could be systematically dismantled.27
The Geopolitical and Environmental Contradictions of Cyber-Physical Systems
While these vulnerabilities empower the right-to-repair movement, they simultaneously expose the catastrophic geopolitical risks of digitizing heavy infrastructure. The same remote access capabilities that allow an OEM to run diagnostics can be easily weaponized. A stark example of this occurred during the conflict in Ukraine, when Russian looters, collaborating with military forces, stole 27 pieces of John Deere farm equipment valued at $5 million from a dealership in Melitopol.28 After the equipment was shipped to Chechnya, the John Deere dealership utilized an in-built remote kill-switch to brick the tractors over the air, rendering them entirely inert.28
While this cinematic comeuppance demonstrates effective anti-theft capabilities, it establishes a terrifying precedent for cyber-physical systems.28 If authorized technicians can remotely paralyze a tractor anywhere in the world, then state-sponsored hacking groups specializing in mass-scale infrastructure attacks could suborn those technicians to execute catastrophic denial-of-service attacks on a nation's agricultural supply chain.28
To defend their monopolies, corporations frequently rely on environmental and safety arguments, claiming that restricting repair access prevents unauthorized modifications that could violate Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) emissions standards or compromise physical safety mechanisms.29 However, these claims are fiercely contested by right-to-repair advocates, who point to the massive environmental cost of forced electronic obsolescence.24 Consequently, legislative interventions are gaining momentum. In the United States, states like New York have passed the Digital Fair Repair Act, while Illinois and others have introduced bills specifically targeting agricultural equipment (e.g., Illinois SB 2669).21 These laws mandate that manufacturers provide public access to detailed repair manuals, diagnostic tools, and parts in accessible formats, fundamentally challenging the monopolistic control of the OEM and elevating the repair manual to a document of consumer enfranchisement.19
Onboarding Friction and the Illusion of Simplicity
The exact moment a user first encounters a disruptive technology is fraught with operational friction. The Quick Start Guide, as a design fiction artifact, captures the vast cognitive and legal chasm between the utopian promises of marketing departments and the immediate, often catastrophic realities of initial implementation.30 By zeroing in on this "Hour 1" onboarding phase, analysts can excavate the deliberate obfuscations embedded within consumer technology and the legal architectures designed to protect corporations from the consequences of their own designs.
The UI of Deception and Hyper-Simplification
The visual and textual layout of a modern onboarding leaflet is an exercise in hyper-simplification. It relies on clean, icon-heavy, three-step processes designed to convey an illusion of frictionless integration.32 This aesthetic choice is not merely an attempt at user-friendliness; it is a calculated psychological mechanism to drive immediate engagement, accelerate time-to-productivity, and reduce the cognitive barrier to entry.32 However, this simplicity actively conceals the complex underlying world models, algorithmic biases, and data-harvesting mechanisms of the device.
Internal product testing reports and UX research frequently reveal that user interfaces (UI) are intentionally designed to mislead, steering users toward default settings that compromise privacy or bypass critical safety configurations to maximize corporate data acquisition.12 When users skip a step, misinterpret a hyper-simplified icon, or fail to understand the underlying logic of the system, the resulting "Hour 1" friction is often devastating. A panicked forum post from a user who improperly calibrated a home biometric security swarm or misunderstood the synchronization of a neural-audio sequencer serves as a primary artifact of this failure. The technology, stripped of its complex context by the Quick Start Guide, becomes a vector for unintended harm.
Product Liability, Design Defects, and the Fine Print
The legal architecture surrounding these consumer products is meticulously designed to entirely contradict the marketing narrative.6 While the glossy exterior of the box promises seamless enhancement of daily life, the obscure liability text printed in 4pt font on the interior serves as an impenetrable shield for the corporation.6 Modern product liability law requires courts to differentiate between manufacturing defects (unintended errors in the physical creation of a single item), marketing defects (inadequate warnings or instructions), and design defects (inherent flaws present in every unit produced according to the plan).33
In the realm of speculative digital goods and software, the boundary between a design defect and user error is purposefully blurred.34 Corporations employ teams of technical writers and legal experts to craft end-user license agreements (EULAs) that absolve them of responsibility for catastrophic outcomes.35 This is increasingly relevant as liability law struggles to adapt to artificial intelligence. For instance, in litigation against the rideshare app Lyft, courts grappled with whether the app's design choices and mass distribution constituted a defective product or merely a service, highlighting the legal ambiguities of software liability.34
The Biden Administration's 2023 National Cybersecurity Strategy has urgently called to shift liability onto entities that fail to take reasonable precautions to secure their software, yet the ex-post nature of products liability means manufacturers are usually only held accountable after a product is released and causes physical injury or property damage.34 If a generative AI platform or an autonomous logistics node fails due to an edge-case scenario not explicitly covered in the hyper-simplified onboarding steps, the corporate liability shield transfers the blame entirely to the user.34
The consequences of these onboarding failures are not merely theoretical; they have massive financial and physical implications.30 A notorious example is the automation of manual invoice matching. While marketed as a foolproof efficiency upgrade utilizing agentic AI, the failure of the underlying algorithms to flag sophisticated deception resulted in catastrophic losses.37 In one prominent case, a man posing as a supplier successfully tricked Google and Facebook into wiring over $100 million in fake invoice payments over two years because the companies relied on automated systems that lacked rigorous checks and balances.37 The Quick Start Guide and the automated onboarding process, therefore, function as mechanisms of plausible deniability, prioritizing rapid adoption over comprehensive user comprehension and systemic security.37

The Commodification of Tomorrow: Catalogs and Grey Markets
The investigation of speculative product catalogs reveals the trajectory of advanced science as it descends into the discount bins of global retail. By treating the catalog as a primary artifact, researchers can unpack the economic realities, the marketing spin, and the illicit secondary lifecycles of commodified futures.39 Projects such as the TBD Catalog produced by the Near Future Laboratory, or their speculative IKEA Catalog developed in collaboration with Stockholm University's Mobile Life Centre, demonstrate how embedding advanced technology in mundane consumer contexts makes secondary effects and behavioral adaptations immediately visible.39
Manufacturing Realities and the Race to the Bottom
The glossy, over-promising pitch of a catalog listing relies on aggressive pricing tiers that suggest widespread technological democratization.42 The TBD Catalog, for example, features simultaneously outlandish yet highly plausible products such as "Oglie: The Social Network for Your Pet," "Anti-Drone Services," and "Data Enlargement Services," presented with the same banal commercial language found in an airplane Skymall magazine.42 Whether advertising 99-cent algorithmic API tokens or wholesale urban projection mapping drones, the catalog presents a future of limitless accessibility.42
However, hitting these ultra-low price points necessitates a deeply exploitative manufacturing process. A bill of lading from an overseas factory accompanying these catalog items reveals the subterranean truth: substandard materials, severe labor exploitation, and a complete disregard for environmental externalities.43 The commodification of the future is built upon a foundation of cheap labor and disposable hardware.43 Consequently, the retail reception of these goods is marked by spectacular failure. Furious 1-star customer reviews document the rapid physical degradation of the products, which fail to meet even a fraction of the catalog's lofty promises.43 Regulatory agencies eventually intervene, issuing cease-and-desist letters regarding unintended side effects, toxic material off-gassing, or algorithmic bias, but usually only after the market has been saturated with the defective commodity.7
The Grey Market and Illicit Repurposing
The most profound insight derived from the analysis of commodified technology is its lifecycle on the secondary market. When a product is cheap, ubiquitous, and lightly regulated, it inevitably finds its way into the "grey market"—a space between the licit supply chain and illicit networks.7 Grey market electronics, heavily traded across borders to exploit pricing disparities (parallel importation), routinely undercut authorized retailers.7 These products often evade local safety and regulatory standards, leaving the end-user vulnerable to defective or non-compliant hardware.7 The rapid expansion of e-commerce has amplified this issue, prompting legislative attempts like the INFORM Consumers Act to impose disclosure requirements on high-volume third-party sellers, though bad actors easily exploit gaps in these regulations to traffic illicit goods.44
Furthermore, mundane consumer technologies are frequently diverted for highly illegal, unintended use-cases.45 In environments characterized by extreme resource constraints or heavy regulation, such as the prison system, everyday items assume disproportionate importance.45 Sociological studies, such as Gibson-Light's research in Arizona prisons, reveal how mundane items like tins of tuna, kimchi bowls, or packages of oatmeal become prized units of exchange, forming the backbone of an illicit currency system.45 Prisoners frequently demonstrate profound agency by ingeniously refashioning these mundane physical objects to advance illicit agendas, such as constructing heating coils from basic electronics or repurposing containers for the transportation and concealment of narcotics.47
This phenomenon of illicit repurposing reaches its apex in modern asymmetrical warfare.48 Unconventional forces and non-state actors routinely repurpose cheap, commercially available technologies to alter strategic battlefield dynamics.49 The widespread availability of consumer drones, originally marketed for aerial photography, has fundamentally shifted modern conflict; these devices are easily modified to deliver improvised explosive devices (IEDs) or conduct advanced reconnaissance, operating alongside anti-tank guided missiles (ATGMs) and man-portable air defense systems (MANPADS).49
The integration of Western commercial electronics into foreign military hardware is pervasive. For instance, the HESA Shahed 136 drone heavily utilizes Western components.50 The same inertial navigation systems, optical recognition hardware, and high-density batteries that power civilian smartphones are extracted and integrated into crude but highly effective autonomous weapons systems.50 This modularity mimics the open software architectures of companies like Tesla, which intentionally designs vehicles to allow rapid replacement of subsystems with cheaper, commercially available alternatives.51 Thus, a fragmented manifest from a grey-market reseller buying 99-cent algorithmic tokens in bulk does not represent a retail success; it represents a critical international security vulnerability.8 The true legacy of a speculative product is not its glossy catalog debut, but its physical state after sitting in a bargain bin, waiting to be cannibalized for its optical sensors by an underground syndicate or a non-state militia.
The Epistolary Architecture of Sociotechnical Speculation
To effectively excavate these deep, messy, and contradictory layers of reality, design fiction relies heavily on a specific narrative and methodological framework: the epistolary format.5 By constructing worldbuilding narratives entirely out of "found documents"—such as leaked emails, diagnostic logs, marginalized scripts, therapy notes, and redacted HR files—the practice entirely removes the omniscient narrator, forcing the documents to speak only for themselves.52
Constructing Verisimilitude Through Artifacts
The epistolary tradition possesses deep literary roots, tracing back to the 17th and 18th centuries with works such as Aphra Behn’s Love Letters Between a Nobleman and His Sister (1684) and Samuel Richardson’s immensely popular Pamela (1740) and Clarissa (1748).52 These early novels utilized the conceit of the "found document" to establish credibility, psychological insight, and deep realism, making readers feel privy to secret, private correspondences.52 This format evolved through the Gothic era, manifesting in works like Horace Walpole's The Castle of Otranto and Bram Stoker’s Dracula, which layered ship's logs, doctor's notes, and telegrams to build tension and verisimilitude.52 Even antiquarian texts like Thomas Amory's Memoirs utilized the found-document frame to offer a material explanation for fragmentary, non-linear narratives.56
In modern design fiction, this literary tradition translates into the meticulous, aesthetic replication of contemporary bureaucratic, legal, and technical formats.55 A sense of deep verisimilitude is achieved by mimicking the specific vernacular of the medium: the truncated syntax of a frantic text message, the sterile formatting of an automated diagnostic printout, the heavily annotated margins of a prop designer's script, or the clinical detachment of a psychologist's therapy notes.53 This "artifact fiction" is broadly categorized into two modes: the outsider point of view (formal, outward-facing documents like newspaper articles or court cases) and the insider point of view (intimate, private communications like emails or letters).9 By layering these documents, designers force the audience to sift through competing and sometimes contradictory accounts, elevating the reader from a passive observer to an active investigator.53
Ambiguity, Agonism, and the Suspension of Disbelief
The true methodological power of artifact fiction lies in its deliberate preservation of ambiguity. A real archaeological excavation is never complete; it is always a fragmented collection of competing voices and incomplete data.53 By embedding a contradiction for every claim and a hidden cost for every technological victory, design fiction creates a highly effective "discursive space" for critical reflection.16
The "NoPlace Notebook" project, developed by researchers Mark Blythe and Enrique Encinas, provides a masterclass in this methodology.58 Tasked by Mozilla to provoke creative thinking around the privacy implications of always-on, voice-activated home routers (akin to Alexa or Google Home), the researchers created an imaginary design workbook.58 They attributed this "found document"—titled "A Bestiary of Routers"—to a fictional persona: Zhang Wei, a brilliant but reclusive designer whose notebook was purportedly "found in an airport".58 Filled with semi-legible sketches, invented languages, and odd diagrams, the workbook was accompanied by fictional WhatsApp dialogues documenting its discovery.58
To add further layers of meta-fictional credibility, the researchers utilized fictional Wikipedia entries and interviews detailing the life of Valdis Ozols, an imaginary Latvian historian and sci-fi writer.58 By framing the speculative object through the lens of industrial espionage, forged documents, and fictional personas, the researchers generated "negative thought experiments" (akin to philosophical constructs like The Trolley Problem).58 This forced the audience to confront the ethical conundrums of mass surveillance without the safety net of an overarching, explanatory narrative.58
This epistolary approach actively subverts the consensus-based models of traditional corporate design. Instead of aiming for a frictionless utopian solution, epistolary design fiction embraces "agonism"—the deliberate surfacing of political, social, and cultural friction, refusal, and disagreement.26 When a reader is presented with the sterile, legally cautious OEM workshop manual alongside the grease-stained, handwritten note of an unauthorized mechanic, they are forced to actively confront the systemic inequities of the technology.53 Richmond Wong's distribution of speculative product catalog zines at UC Berkeley open houses similarly utilized this technique, presenting fictional surveillance devices (like "ChildTrack for Advertisers") to spark unprompted, reflective conversations about social values, privacy, and the potential for technological harm in cases of intimate partner violence.59 The lack of traditional narrative framing forces the audience to reconstruct the socio-technical reality themselves, thereby deeply internalizing the critique.53
Synthesis and Structural Implications
The meticulous excavation of design fiction idioms—film, repair manuals, quick start guides, and product catalogs—reveals a unified underlying thesis regarding technological advancement: the future is not defined by the pristine, frictionless deployment of innovation, but by the intense operational friction of its implementation and the inevitability of its physical decay.
The analysis demonstrates that cinematic diegetic prototypes, while highly effective at envisioning posthumanist integration and advanced urban environments, inherently mask the exploitative labor practices of the VFX industry and the persistent physical limitations of fabrication.15 The right-to-repair movement highlights how OEM monopolization and DRM frameworks transform basic infrastructure into a legal battleground, inevitably driving end-users toward sophisticated, unauthorized black markets and jailbroken firmware to maintain operational sovereignty over their own machinery.20
The hyper-simplified Quick Start Guide functions not as a tool for user empowerment, but as a calculated mechanism for UI deception and a legal shield designed to transfer the massive liability of systemic design defects entirely onto the consumer.6 Finally, the product catalog exposes the dark, subterranean lifecycle of commodification, wherein cheap manufacturing and exploited labor inevitably fuel a massive grey market. In this space, mundane, highly accessible commercial technologies are seamlessly repurposed for illicit and often catastrophic military or subterranean applications, completely bypassing international regulatory frameworks.43
Through the strategic use of epistolary formats and the meticulous construction of found documents, design fiction transcends mere futurist forecasting. It operates as an urgent, uncompromising infrastructural audit of the present.5 By confronting the audience with the raw, intact, and contradictory artifacts of speculative systems—whether a heavily redacted technical log, a frantic forum post, or a grease-stained workaround—the discipline demands a critical reevaluation of the legal, economic, and operational frameworks that currently govern technological development. The artifacts excavated from these fictional futures serve as an indispensable warning regarding the fragile, highly contested, and fundamentally unsustainable trajectory of our own socio-technical reality.
